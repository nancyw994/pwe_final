Who Gets Impacted Unequally

AI food recommendations may seem convenient, but they can quietly reinforce inequality. Schanzenbach and Pitts (2020) found that lower-income users ordered more fast food through delivery apps due to local economic conditions and fewer choices. Pricing Disparities: Higher Costs for Some Neighborhoods

Food delivery is supposed to be convenient, but that convenience comes at a higher cost for some communities.

  • Delivery Fees: In general, low-income ZIP codes tend to have higher fees due to fewer drivers and limited restaurant options nearby.
  • Surge Pricing: During peak times (nights, weekends, bad weather), surge pricing kicks in. These increases have hit low-driver regions the hardest, even though these users may have few affordable food options left.

The Brookings Institution warns that these price gaps widen food inequality, especially for those already struggling with food insecurity (George & Tomer, 2022). It's not just about convenience, it's about fairness.
