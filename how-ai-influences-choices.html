The Mechanism

How AI Influences Choices

Most of us think we make food choices either because something is delicious, we are in a mood for a certain cuisine, or it's a convenient option. But behind the menu, AI quietly shapes those decisions. Food delivery apps, social media feeds, and digital menus use algorithms to predict and influence your cravings. From the meals visible first to ones labeled "most popular," AI systems guide choices by learning your behavior and prioritizing what produces clicks or profits. This invisible influence may seem helpful but raises some serious questions about autonomy, manipulation, and control over the menu you never asked for.

What Is a Recommender System?

A recommender system is a type of artificial intelligence that uses different information to predict what content or items a user is most likely to choose. These systems are at the core of platforms like TikTok, YouTube, Spotify, Instagram, Uber Eats, and DoorDash. Klimenta (2023) shows how recommender systems work by analyzing the user's behavior, such as what you search for, click on, or pause over, to decide what to show you next.

In food platforms, these systems decide which restaurants appear first, what meals are featured, and which dishes are promoted. They are not objective or neutral menus. They are carefully curated lists based on predictions and profit incentives.

What Inputs Feed the Algorithm?

When you open a food app, you are not randomly seeing restaurants. Instead, you see a list ranked by a machine learning algorithm. These algorithms weigh hundreds of data points including your location, time of day, previous orders, spending habits, dietary tags, delivery speed, and what others like you have chosen. The goal is to optimize for clicks, convenience, and cost-efficiency, instead of supporting necessarily nutritional value, local business support, or variety.

Recommender systems use a mix of techniques to predict what you'll want next. Commonly used techniques are collaborative, content-based, and context-aware filtering.

Three Filtering Methods

Collaborative (Ricci et al., 2015)
Similar users → Similar foods
Example: Friends binge hotpot videos → You see hotpot deals.
Content-Based (Ricci et al., 2015)
Food attributes → Similar items
Example: Favorite spicy ramen → App suggests other chili-heavy bowls.
Context-Aware (Adomavicius & Tuzhilin, 2015)
Time/Location → Relevant suggestions
Example: Open app at 8 AM near campus → Breakfast burritos appear first.

The Filter-Bubble Effect

These recommender systems often reinforce your preexisting preferences. For example, when you watch a Korean BBQ video on TikTok, the app might show you more similar videos. As time goes on, similar options become more prevalent and other food varieties become less noticeable. Repetition of content reinforces limited preferences, a phenomenon known as a "filter bubble" (Popken, B., 2019).

Why It Matters

These recommender systems don't just shape what food you see. They can also reinforce social and economic divides. In low-income neighborhoods, users may get fewer healthy options, longer wait times, or higher fees. And it's not just consumers, delivery workers are also impacted by AI systems that track their speed, acceptance rate, and customer reviews, affecting their income and job security (Rosenblat, 2018).
